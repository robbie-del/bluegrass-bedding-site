The Respiratory Health Problem

Dust in the Stall is a Performance Problem

Inflammatory Airway Disease (IAD) is one of the most common performance-limiting conditions in racehorses. Unlike full-blown heaves, IAD presents subclinically — horses appear healthy but show measurable reductions in VO₂ max, respiratory efficiency, and recovery rate.

The primary driver? Respirable dust particles in the stall environment — specifically PM2.5 to PM10 particles that penetrate deep into airway tissue. The main source of those particles is the bedding itself.

A horse spends 20+ hours a day in its stall. If that stall air is loaded with respirable dust, the cumulative airway impact is significant — and measurable in race day performance.

Our Process

Triple Screening: How It Actually Works

Most bedding is screened once — if at all. We screen three times, at three different mesh grades, to systematically eliminate every category of dust particle.

1
Coarse Screen — 8 mesh

Removes oversized debris, bark fragments, and foreign material. Ensures product uniformity before the second screen. Targets particles above 2.36mm.

2
Medium Screen — 20 mesh

Targets mid-range particles between 0.85mm–2.36mm. This is the range most single-screen products miss entirely — the particles that become airborne during stall activity.

3
Fine Screen — 50 mesh

The critical final pass. Targets sub-0.3mm particles — the respirable PM2.5–PM10 range that penetrates deep into equine airway tissue and drives IAD development.

Regulatory Framework

HISA & the Future of Barn Standards

The Horseracing Integrity and Safety Authority is moving the industry toward higher, more consistent welfare standards — and that includes the stall environment. HISA's welfare rules already address medication, track safety, and veterinary protocols. Barn air quality and bedding material standards are a logical next step.

Barns that are already using low-dust, pure-pine bedding from a dedicated equine manufacturer aren't just doing the right thing for their horses — they're ahead of the regulatory curve. When standards tighten, the answer is already in the stall.
